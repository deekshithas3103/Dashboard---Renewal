Bright Spot

SLH had the highest renewal rate

1 of 4 SLH customers (25%) renewed — the highest rate of any Ohio tier. Small sample, but worth noting: SLH is the newer product family and it's outperforming the established tiers in this state. Whatever's working with these customers deserves investigation before we scale it.

SLH: 25.0% · tedBook: 17.6% · Overall: 18.2%
Concern

tedBook drives the volume but mediocre results

17 of 22 customers (77%) are on tedBook, but only 3 renewed. More concerning: 7 of 17 (41%) explicitly said no. This isn't a silence problem for tedBook — it's an active rejection problem. Customers tried it, looked at it, and chose not to come back.

tedBook: 41% explicit non-renewal · The largest cohort, with the largest exit signal
Headline

Active rejection, not silence

45% of Ohio customers explicitly declined renewal — well above the "No Response" rate. This is different from a typical churn pattern. We had conversations with these customers; they just chose to leave. The win-back opportunity here is real because we know who they are.

Not Renewing (45%) outpaces No Response (36%) — only state in the dataset where this is true
Pattern

Renewals scattered across the year

The 4 Ohio renewals came in Jul, Feb, Mar, and Jun — no clear seasonal cluster. Compare this to Indiana where both renewals were Aug/Feb. Ohio's renewal motion likely depends more on individual relationships than calendar timing.

No single renewal month dominated · Implication: relationship-driven, not cycle-driven

The Looming Risk for SY 2026–27

Risk Alert · SY 2026–27 Renewal at Risk

The Ohio churn pattern is active rejection — and we don't know why.

In 24–25, 10 of 22 Ohio customers (45%) explicitly told us they wouldn't renew. This is unusual — most states lose customers to silence. Ohio loses them to conversations that don't go our way. Without understanding the reasons behind those 10 declines, we can't design an effective 26–27 retention motion. The 25–26 cohort is heading toward the same outcome unless we intervene now.

Critical Gap

Ten "no" answers we never investigated

The single most important data we don't have: why those 10 Ohio customers said no. Were they unhappy with the product? Budget cuts? District-level decision? Without exit interviews or feedback collection, we keep selling the same way and getting the same result.

10 explicit declines · 0 documented reasons
Compounding Issue

SLH customers are completely unrostered

7 SLH customers acquired in 25–26 — and zero have been rostered. This is especially concerning because SLH was the best-renewing tier in 24–25 (25%). If we lose these customers to activation failure, we lose the very segment that's been working.

SLH 25–26: 7 acquired · 0 rostered · 1 of 4 renewals achieved
What History Tells Us

Active rejection often hides product-fit issues

When 45% of customers explicitly leave, it usually points to something specific — pricing, product-state-standards alignment, district leadership change, or competitive displacement. Identifying which one is happening in Ohio is the highest-leverage research we can do before 26–27.

Pattern: deliberate departure → root cause likely identifiable
The Opening

29% rostering is real momentum

Unlike states with zero activation, Ohio has 6 customers already on the platform. If we double that by January and run training programs through the spring, the 26–27 conversation looks very different — we'll have actual usage data to point to for at least a third of the cohort.

Current: 6 of 21 rostered · Target: 15 of 21 by Jan 2026

SY 2026–27 Strategic Plan

Six moves built around Ohio's specific pattern: explicit-rejection-dominant churn, SLH outperforming tedBook, and a 25–26 cohort that's partially activated.

1

Run exit interviews on the 10 declines

Before designing the 26–27 motion, we need to understand why 10 customers explicitly declined. Structured exit interviews with all 10 from 24–25, plus the 17 still-open 25–26 renewals. The answers will reveal whether the gap is product, price, district policy, or competition.

Target: 10 interviews complete by Oct 2025
2

Double down on SLH — Ohio's bright spot

SLH had the highest 24–25 renewal rate (25%) and has already produced 1 of 4 renewals in 25–26. But all 7 new SLH customers are unrostered. White-glove the SLH cohort: dedicated CSM, in-person training, weekly check-ins until activation.

SLH: 25% renewal rate · 7 of 7 new customers unrostered
3

Get the remaining 15 rostered by January

6 of 21 already rostered — keep the momentum. Direct CSM outreach to the 15 still off-platform, with priority on the 8 spring acquisitions (Apr–May). Without rostering, the customer enters renewal with no felt experience of the product.

Current: 6 of 21 rostered · Target: 15+ by Jan 31, 2026
4

Win-back campaign for the 10 decliners

Unlike silent customers, the 10 who explicitly declined are knowable and reachable. Build a structured win-back outreach for SY 26–27 enrollment — addressed by name, referencing their stated reasons (once collected). Even a 20% win-back rate would add 2 renewals.

Target: 2 win-backs from the 24–25 decline cohort
5

Address the tedBook problem head-on

tedBook is 77% of the Ohio cohort but only renews at 17.6%, and 41% of tedBook customers explicitly declined. Whatever's working nationally for tedBook isn't translating in Ohio. Likely candidates: state assessment alignment, district workflow fit, or sales positioning.

tedBook: 17 customers · 7 declined · 7 silent · only 3 renewed
6

Build a year-round renewal cadence

Ohio renewals didn't cluster in any month — they were scattered. That means there's no single renewal season to plan around; it's a year-round relationship motion. Build a quarterly business review cadence with every Ohio customer, regardless of renewal date.

Target: 4 QBRs per customer per year · Starting Aug 2025
The 26–27 Goal
Lift Ohio renewal rate from 18% (baseline) to 40%+ in SY 2026–27 — by understanding why customers leave, doubling down on SLH, and bringing the 25–26 cohort into renewal season with rostering and usage evidence.
